most commonly used lifecycle models: the Waterfall Model, the Agile Model, and the Iterative Model.


4.1 Waterfall model/not my favorite model

The Waterfall Model is one of the oldest and most traditional lifecycle models in development. It follows a linear and sequential approach, where each stage of the development process is completed before moving on to the next. Once a stage is finished, it is considered "locked," and the project cannot move backwards to revise previous stages.




Sequential Process
The project progresses in a step-by-step manner, with distinct phases such as planning, design, implementation, testing, and maintenance.


Clear Milestones
Each stage has specific deliverables that must be completed before moving on to the next phase.


Fixed Requirements
The requirements are typically established at the beginning of the project and remain fixed throughout the process.



4.2 Agile Model

The Agile Model is a much more flexible and adaptive approach to development, and it has gained widespread popularity in industries such as software development. Unlike the Waterfall Model, Agile is iterative, meaning that development occurs in smaller, incremental cycles known as sprints. Each sprint delivers a working version of the product that can be reviewed, tested, and improved upon in the next iteration.


Iterative Cycles (Sprints)
Development is broken into small, manageable cycles, each focused on delivering specific features or improvements.


Frequent Feedback
Regular testing and feedback are integral to the process, allowing teams to adapt to changes and continuously refine the product.


Collaboration
Agile encourages close collaboration between developers, testers, and stakeholders, ensuring that the project evolves to meet the user’s needs.



4.3 Iterative Model

The Iterative Model is somewhat of a middle ground between the Waterfall and Agile models. In this approach, the project is developed through repeated cycles or iterations. Each iteration represents a mini-development lifecycle, where a portion of the system is developed, tested, and improved before moving on to the next iteration.








Repeated Cycles
The project is developed in iterations, with each cycle refining the previous work and adding new functionality.


Progressive Development
With each iteration, the system becomes more fully developed, allowing for improvements and refinements along the way.


Flexibility
Like Agile, the Iterative Model allows for adjustments as new requirements or issues emerge, but it maintains a more structured approach than Agile.


For large, complex projects with well-established requirements, the Waterfall Model may be the best choice. Its structured approach ensures that all aspects of the project are planned and documented in detail, minimising confusion or scope creep.

For smaller, more agile projects where collaboration and adaptability are key, the Agile Model offers greater flexibility and speed. It allows teams to respond to changing requirements and customer feedback in real time.

Flexibility and Change

If you anticipate changes to the project requirements or expect the scope to evolve, Agile is the most suitable model. Its iterative nature allows for frequent course corrections and prioritises responding to new information.

The Iterative Model can also accommodate change, though it is more structured than Agile. It allows for adjustments at the end of each iteration but retains a clearer framework than Agile’s dynamic cycles.

Stakeholder Involvement

Projects that require frequent stakeholder feedback and input are better suited to Agile or Iterative models. These models allow for regular check-ins with stakeholders and adjustments based on their input, ensuring that the final product aligns with their expectations.

In contrast, the Waterfall Model is more appropriate for projects with less stakeholder involvement, where requirements are clearly defined at the outset, and little input is needed throughout the development process.

Risk Management

The Waterfall Model manages risk by ensuring that every stage is thoroughly planned and documented before moving on to the next phase. This works best when risks are known upfront and can be accounted for in the initial planning stages.

Agile and Iterative models manage risk by being adaptable. They allow teams to identify risks early in the process and address them in each iteration or sprint, reducing the likelihood of major issues later in the project.



software development life cycle
(SDLC) is the process of planning, writing, modifying, and maintaining software.


Stage 1: Plan and brainstorm.

Stage 2: Analyze requirements.

Stage 4: Develop the code.

Stage 5: Test the product.

Stage 6: Implement and launch the product.

Stage 7: Set up maintenance and operations.


V-shaped model: Also called the verification and validation model, the V-shaped model allows for simultaneous development and testing. Like Waterfall, this model follows a linear progression, but you only move on to the next stage once the team finishes the previous one. The V-shaped model focuses on documentation and planning, so it's ideal for large-scale projects with long schedules. However, the rigidity built into the system only allows for infrequent changes.


Big Bang model: Compared to other software development models, Big Bang has less structure. With this model, developers start working with little more than an understanding of the project requirements. They must figure things out as they go along, as they put most of the resources into the software development stage. Big Bang focuses on getting something working quickly. This approach works well with small projects, where one or two developers can work together to determine requirements and solutions as they code. However, it can be expensive and time-consuming for large projects.


Spiral model: The spiral model combines elements of other models, namely Waterfall and iterative. Developers work in shorter cycles, and the work within the cycles follows a linear progression. After each iteration, the software gradually gets better. The key advantage of this model is that it helps manage risk very effectively by focusing on small portions of risk at a time and using different approaches based on the risk profile at that stage. This allows developers to make adjustments without compromising the project's outcome. This approach works well in highly complex, large, expensive projects.
